Optimal Strategy in Darts Cricket

A computational study of 22 strategies across skill levels, bull accuracy, and opponent matchups

Extending Frongello (2018) — 2026

The Study

Cricket is one of the most popular darts games played in bars, leagues, and tournaments worldwide. Two players take turns throwing three darts at seven targets (15–20 and Bull). Close all seven and outscore your opponent to win. The strategic tension — when to score points vs. when to close numbers — is the central question of the game.

In 2018, Andrew Frongello published the first systematic analysis, defining 17 strategies and testing them in simulation. We extended that work with realistic skill profiles, new experimental strategies, and a dimension Frongello didn't model: bull accuracy. The result is the most comprehensive computational study of darts cricket strategy to date.

Key Findings

  1. 01 The right strategy depends on your skill level. There is no single best strategy. At low MPR (beginners), strategy differences are small and luck dominates. At mid MPR (league players), E2 (Honeypot) and S2 (Score Then Cover) compete closely. At high MPR (pros), Phase Switch pulls ahead. The optimal approach shifts across the full skill spectrum.
  2. 02 Bull accuracy is a hidden dimension that changes everything. When we modeled bull as harder to hit than numbers (which it is on a real board), the entire strategy landscape shifted. E1 (Early Bull) — previously the #1 strategy at 9 of 11 skill levels — collapsed to dead last at MPR 4.0+. A single parameter change reshuffled the competitive field more than any other factor in the study.
  3. 03 Your optimal strategy depends on your opponent's stats too. Asymmetric bull accuracy between players creates win rate spreads of 16–39 percentage points (depending on skill level) and changes which strategy is optimal. Strategy recommendations change in ~77% of asymmetric slots. A player who is great at bull should play fundamentally differently than one who isn't — and differently again depending on their opponent's bull accuracy.
  4. 04 Frongello's core principles hold across all conditions. Score first, then cover. Never chase. Weaker players want shorter games. These findings replicate across 11 skill levels and 16 bull conditions. An RL agent independently rediscovered these principles through pure trial and error, providing strong confirmation they reflect genuine strategic truth.
  5. 05 A new strategy beats everything at high skill. Phase Switch — score aggressively early, then irreversibly commit to closing — ranks #1 from MPR 3.6 upward and beats S2 (Score Then Cover) head-to-head from ~3.0. The key innovation is eliminating oscillation: once you decide to close out, you never go back. Its advantage grows with skill, reaching +5.0pp vs S2 at pro level.
  6. 06 An RL agent discovered something no human designed. A branching actor-critic agent, trained from scratch against 13 hard bots, learned to adapt its opening based on turn order: 20 when going first, 18 when going second. None of the 28 hand-crafted strategies make this distinction. The agent also independently confirmed Frongello's "never chase" and "score before cover" principles.

What Should You Play?

The answer depends on three things: your skill, your bull accuracy, and your opponent. Here's the simplified regime map from 1,936 tournaments:

Your Profile Best Strategy Why
Low MPR (beginner) S2 (Score Then Cover) or E2 (Honeypot) Misses dominate; simple score-then-cover maximizes what you can control
Mid MPR (league) E2 (Honeypot), S2, or PS (Phase Switch) The competitive field is tight; E2's honeypot and S2's universality trade the #1 spot
High MPR (pro) PS (Phase Switch) Aggressive early scoring + irreversible switch exploits accuracy advantage
Bad at bull (0.25–0.50x) S9 (Max Threshold) or E2 (Honeypot) High threshold (S9) defers bull longest; E2's honeypot avoids bull-dependent decisions
Good at bull (1.00x) E1 (Early Bull) Early bull closure becomes an offensive weapon when you can hit it
Outmatched on skill S2 (Score Then Cover) or S1 (Pure Close) S2 wins head-to-head against any standard opponent. S1 is the safer maximin choice if you don't know your opponent's strategy — it has the best worst-case and highest tournament average for the weaker player
